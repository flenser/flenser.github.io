## Chapter One: *"The Clod of Earth Awaits the Snow"* — Part One

{: .highlight }
> **Translator's note**
>
> Kai's full name, Chitaira Kai, written with the kanji 地平塊, means "clod of earth on flat ground" — or more evocatively, a lump of earth on the open plain. The chapter title "The Clod of Earth Awaits the Snow" is not a nature image but a name: it is Kai who waits. What she is waiting for, and what the snow represents, the story has yet to tell us.

Among the countless memories she had made with Kai — more than she could ever count — this day was the one that could only be called the origin.\
No matter how far she searched for somewhere to lay the blame, she could find nothing that satisfied.\
And so perhaps that day had simply been destined to be the day she and Kai met.

She was still gripping the steering wheel, still dragging behind her the fact that she had gotten angry when a colleague teased her about being overprotective. Even between people who knew each other well, having someone make a joke at you when you were already flustered — she had reacted too strongly. The whole way here she had been replaying that moment over and over, as if running an investigation.\
While she was at it, she began gradually to think: *had she really been that angry... maybe she'd just raised her voice a little...* — finding, by degrees, a more self-serving place to land. She made an effort to stop thinking about it. There was nothing to be gained from going further.\
She could apologise tomorrow, when she got to work. That was all there was to it.\
More than that — right now, she needed to be thinking about her daughter.\
The light changed, and she could see the car at the front of the queue finally beginning, slowly, to move. She glanced sideways at a wave of foreign tourists who had poured off a bus and filled the pavement, and eased the car forward.\
She had always liked, for some reason she couldn't explain, the arcing traces the windscreen wipers left behind. Since she was a child.\
The grey weather that had settled in at the end of September had been holding since yesterday. The rain fell at its own pace — neither hurrying nor stopping — quietly, steadily, making its sound. The dim light and the slight drop in temperature: her daughter would probably find it easier to sleep like this.\
Her daughter had been home sick with a fever for two days. Of course she had worried even while she was at work — it was only natural — and today she had chosen to leave much earlier than usual. Someone might think: *at her age, middle school already* — but even if she was in her second year of middle school, to a parent, a child was always a child.\
*And that's exactly what my colleague pointed out* — the regret started going around in circles again. She shook it off and made herself focus on the road.\
She took a slight detour, got the shopping done, and returned home. She opened the garage and parked in the usual spot. Her husband walked to work with the dog; since the whole family had stopped going out together much, the car had effectively become hers. She hooked the supermarket bag over her hand so she wouldn't forget it, and headed for the front door.\
She unlocked the door and stepped inside. No sounds; dim. The darkened hallway felt quietly forlorn. Her daughter might be sleeping — she kept her footsteps soft as she pushed open the bedroom door and looked in. In the narrow gap, her daughter stirred at the intrusion, and met her eyes from where she lay.\
"I'm home. Were you asleep?"\
"I was dozing, but the sound of the lock woke me up."\
She crouched beside her. "Welcome back," her daughter said — and laughed.\
"What is it?"\
"Just — Mum always comes home early when I'm sick."\
"In that case, it might be better for your health if I came home late every night."\
In truth that was already how it was, so she really could only be grateful to her husband.\
"Your temperature? Have you checked?"\
"Before lunch — it was around 37, so it's better than yesterday."\
Yesterday her daughter had been complaining about her throat and even talking seemed like an effort, so just hearing her speak normally was the first thing that put her at ease. In the closed room the lingering summer heat was slowly pressing in, the fan turning weakly, stirring the air in lazy loops. She was trying to decide whether to ask her daughter if she was too warm or too cold when her arm moved and the supermarket bag crinkled, and she remembered.\
"Hold on."\
She stepped out of the room and went to the kitchen. She had stopped on the way home — now she transferred what she'd bought into a bowl, and for no particular reason caught sight of herself reflected faintly in the ventilation window, and reached up to touch a strand of her hair.\
When had it stopped feeling strange to see black-haired herself in a mirror. By now, the only people in her life who had known her in her original colour were her husband and her parents. Carrying that small, idle nostalgia with her, she finished getting things ready and returned to her daughter's room.\
She held out a yellow bowl straight away — tinned mandarin segments in syrup.\
"Whenever you have a fever you always want this."\
She thought she might be at the age where being treated like a child would earn a protest — but her daughter's face softened readily, and she felt relieved.\
"Muuum, I can't be bothered getting up, feed me~"\
"I don't mind, but are you sure?"\
Her daughter, still wrapped in her duvet, eyes bright with excitement and anticipation, nose slightly flushed. She wasn't sure this was a good idea, but she scooped up a mandarin and some syrup with the spoon and held it out toward her daughter's mouth.\
Her daughter stretched her neck forward eagerly — but.\
"*Bfuhhh.*"\
"There you go."\
It should have been fairly obvious what would happen if you tried to get a spoonful of mandarin and syrup into someone lying completely flat. Predictably, the syrup spilled from the corner of her mouth and her face was a mess. And she choked.\
She wiped her daughter's face and pillow, then handed over the spoon and bowl.\
"Don't be lazy. Sit up and eat."\
"Aye aye~"\
Her daughter, lips and cheeks damp, reluctantly pulled herself upright. Her temperature and her mood both seemed to have recovered considerably. Yesterday her husband had been the one looking after her, and he had messaged to say her fever wasn't coming down — he'd been worried, and so had she. Two days in a row felt like too much to leave to him, and she hadn't been able to settle anyway, so today she had left early.\
"Do you think you'll be able to eat something for dinner?"\
"Hmm... I'd recommend something easy on the stomach."\
"What a precise diagnosis. If you think you can eat... though I won't be the one making it."\
It wasn't that she couldn't cook, but her husband's way of putting it was *a matter of suitability*, and her daughter's way of putting it was something like *Mum's cooking tastes a bit thin*. And more specifically, she'd been told her seasoning was too light. But when she consciously tried to make it stronger, the flavours came out discordant instead. That sense of balance — she had simply never acquired it. She had been telling herself ever since they married that she would have her husband teach her one day, and fourteen years had passed.\
Then the doorbell sounded, announcing a visitor. She exchanged a look with her daughter.\
"Not Dad, right?"\
"Probably a bit early for him."\
Who on earth would come on a rainy day like this — she went to answer, mentally ruling out a delivery since she'd requested weekend slots for those, and glanced without particular expectation at what was showing on the intercom screen.\
For a moment, her breath stopped.\
Something struck the centre of her chest, and before she could make sense of what had hit her, her eyes had already contracted.
